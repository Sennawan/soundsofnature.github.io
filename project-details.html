Project Report on Preventing Reclamation of Muthurajawela Wetland

Introduction

Muthurajawela Wetland, located near Negombo in Sri Lanka, is one of the most significant and largest coastal wetlands in the country. Spanning over 6,000 hectares, it serves as a natural buffer, protecting inland areas from flooding and supporting an array of biodiversity. Wetlands like Muthurajawela play an essential role in climate regulation, flood control, and providing habitat for numerous species.
However, this unique ecosystem faces an alarming threat due to land reclamation, which is driven by rapid urbanization and industrial development. These reclamation activities not only threaten the ecological balance but also the livelihoods of local communities. This project, initiated by the "Sounds of Nature" organization, aims to raise awareness and mobilize efforts to prevent the destruction of Muthurajawela Wetland through reclamation.

Objectives

The primary objectives of this project are:
  • Raise Awareness: Educate the public and policymakers about the critical importance of Muthurajawela Wetland and the detrimental impacts of land reclamation.
  • Prevent Further Reclamation: Collaborate with local and international environmental agencies to halt ongoing reclamation activities and prevent future attempts.
  • Promote Conservation: Restore and rehabilitate areas that have been affected by reclamation, ensuring long-term protection for the wetlandâ€™s ecosystem.
  • Advocate for Policy Enforcement: Strengthen legal protections for the wetland by advocating for stricter enforcement of environmental laws.

Current Issues and Threats

  • Land Reclamation: Over recent years, large portions of Muthurajawela Wetland have been illegally reclaimed for residential and industrial purposes. The wetland is being filled with earth and other debris to create space for construction, which significantly reduces the area available for natural water retention.
  • Environmental Degradation: The reclamation activities have caused the loss of habitat for various wildlife species, including rare birds, amphibians, and reptiles. The disappearance of these habitats has cascading effects on the food web and the overall ecosystem.
  • Pollution: Along with reclamation, pollution from nearby urban areas, industries, and agricultural runoff has increased, further degrading the wetland. This pollution not only affects the water quality but also threatens the health of the communities that rely on the wetland for sustenance.

Impact of Land Reclamation

  • On Biodiversity: Muthurajawela Wetland is home to many species, some of which are endemic and endangered. The loss of habitat due to land reclamation has resulted in a decrease in species populations, particularly bird species that rely on the wetland for breeding and feeding.
  • On Flooding: The wetland functions as a natural flood control system, absorbing excess water during heavy rains. As the area of the wetland decreases due to reclamation, the risk of flooding in surrounding communities increases significantly, endangering lives and property.
  • On Fisheries and Livelihoods: Many local communities depend on the wetland for fishing and agriculture. The degradation of the wetland has led to a decline in fish populations, affecting the livelihoods of those who rely on these natural resources.
  • On Climate Regulation: Wetlands act as carbon sinks, storing large amounts of carbon that help mitigate climate change. The destruction of Muthurajawela Wetland reduces its capacity to absorb carbon, contributing to global warming and environmental instability.

Legal and Policy Framework

Sri Lanka has several environmental regulations aimed at protecting natural habitats, including the National Environmental Act and the Flora and Fauna Protection Ordinance. However, gaps in enforcement have allowed illegal reclamation activities to continue.
  • National Legislation: While Muthurajawela is recognized as an ecologically significant area, stronger policies need to be enacted to prevent further reclamation and to penalize those who engage in illegal activities.
  • International Conventions: As a signatory to the Ramsar Convention on Wetlands, Sri Lanka is obligated to protect its wetlands. Strengthening the countryâ€™s compliance with international agreements can help drive better conservation efforts.

Action Plan

  1. Awareness Campaigns:
    • Launch educational workshops in schools and local communities to highlight the ecological and economic importance of Muthurajawela.
    • Use social media platforms to reach a wider audience, sharing documentaries, infographics, and real-time updates on the status of the wetland.
  2. Petition for Government Intervention:
    • Collaborate with environmental groups and legal experts to create petitions and push for stronger legal protections.
    • Meet with government officials and environmental policymakers to advocate for the enforcement of existing regulations and propose new policies.
  3. Partnerships:
    1. Establish partnerships with international conservation organizations such as the World Wildlife Fund (WWF) and Wetlands International to gain financial and technical support.
    2. Work with academic institutions to conduct scientific studies on the environmental and economic impacts of land reclamation.
  4. Monitoring and Reporting:
    1. Develop a community-based monitoring system where local volunteers report illegal activities and environmental changes in the wetland.
      3. Collaborate with local media outlets to publicize any ongoing threats to the wetland, creating public pressure for action.
  5. Rehabilitation Programs:
    • Initiate tree-planting and habitat restoration programs to rehabilitate areas of the wetland that have already been damaged.
    • Work with environmental scientists to restore natural water flow and reintroduce native species where possible.

Expected Outcomes

  • Halt in Reclamation: By raising awareness and strengthening legal frameworks, the project aims to put a stop to ongoing reclamation activities and prevent future efforts.
  • Biodiversity Conservation: Protecting the wetland will safeguard the habitats of countless species, promoting biodiversity and ecological balance.
  • Flood Risk Mitigation: Preserving the wetland will ensure that it continues to serve as a natural flood control system, protecting surrounding communities.
  • Sustainable Development: Through public involvement and legal action, the project will help promote sustainable land-use practices that prioritize conservation over unchecked development.

Timeline and Milestones

  • Short-term (1-3 months): Launch awareness campaigns, organize workshops, and establish partnerships with environmental organizations.
    • Medium-term (3-12 months): Initiate petitions, hold meetings with government officials, and begin community monitoring programs. Long-term (12+ months): Begin rehabilitation programs, evaluate the impact of the project, and continue advocacy for stronger wetland protection policies.

Conclusion

The reclamation of land in Muthurajawela Wetland poses a severe threat to its ecological integrity and the livelihoods of surrounding communities. Through the efforts of the "Sounds of Nature" organization, it is possible to halt these destructive practices and work towards restoring and protecting this vital natural resource. By mobilizing public support, advocating for legal enforcement, and restoring damaged areas, we can ensure that Muthurajawela Wetland continues to serve as a critical ecosystem for generations to come.
